    Bounce/Union Station

    LGBTQ+ bar and nightclub.
    Central home for drag in Cleveland.
    Open 2001-2017.


    Oral History by Katie Horowitz



    In Discussion with Andria Michaels


    Andria Michaels is an entertainer based in Columbus, Ohio. Michaels worked as an MC at Bounce for 10 years, and has hosted and performed shows and pageants throughout the state of Ohio for 25+
    Cash App: $Andria1000


      Image courtesy of Our Community Roots


    ANDRIA:  Before Bounce opened, there was a bar called The Cage on Detroit Avenue. That was such a good bar, it was hoppin seven nights a week. I did shows there, and I did shows at a little bar further up the street called Sexx. The owner of Bounce was looking for somebody to MC the shows at Bounce before it even opened. He came to one of my shows up the street and heard me MC, then he asked me if I wanted to come down there and work. And I said yeah. Nobody knew Bounce was gonna be as big as it was and knew the premise behind it. He didn’t understand the need that Cleveland had for a big open space like that for everybody to gather. He didn’t think it was gonna be as successful as it was, I don’t think. And I was there from the time it opened and worked there for ten years.

    In between that time, we used to do shows in the little room on the side called Pump. I had to do two shows a night there on Friday and Saturday, and that place would be packed, stupid packed, an ocean of homosexuals, and you could barely breathe in that room, and I begged him, begged him, please open up the dancefloor side so we can do shows over there. He said okay, if you pack that room for a month, I’ll do it. Three weeks later he had the dance room side open. And Kari Nickels, I begged her to come and work there, and she worked there until they closed. Kari Nickels is one of the foundation members of that damn place. Without her, I don’t think shows would have survived as long as they did there. Bounce was nice because it was for 18 and over, and the fledgling kids got a chance to go, and got a chance to see, and got a chance to experience all of the rich, eclectic gay history and gay community that Cleveland has. And it was the last big bar, big dance bar there in town. Because I really don’t think that they have anything of that magnitude there now, do they?


    LEILA:  From what I understand, drag has just been integrated into different straight bars and venues or smaller LGBTQ+ bars. But there isn’t one central spot to my knowledge.


    ANDRIA:  That’s so sad! There was a time where drag was so wanted and so desired in Cleveland. I worked seven nights a week, and I was doing nine shows a week. I could have done more if I wanted to, but there was just not enough time or days in a month. And everybody made money. Everyone did. The weekend shows at Bounce were phenomenal. They were packed, people just stupid packed in there. The queens made hella money and the atmosphere was great. It was straight, gay, bisexual, didn’t matter. Everybody was in there. And Cleveland needed that so bad. The Grid had closed, Numbers had closed, and most of them were downtown. Cleveland was going through this change, this metamorphosis, and Bounce came at a great time and filled such a good need there. I had no idea why the original owner of Bounce had sold the bar or gave it up. I thought it was a great bar and a great time for everybody.




      Andria Michaels and Lady Bunny at Bounce circa 2002. Image courtesy of Scotty Nussbaum.

    Drag was never intended for anyone to make a living off of. And if it was, you’re one of the very few. Now I have managed over the years to make a living off drag, but I’ve done it for so long that I can work where I want to, and bars are very kind to me and I’ve made a name for myself in the 25 years that I’ve been doing it. But a lot of girls now, a lot of the newcomers, a lot of the fledgling girls, what are they gonna do? Where are they gonna hone their crafts at? You have to be able to give some stage time before anyone will pay you to get on stage, but it’s a catch-22 now, especially with Corona. Nobody’s gonna give you a chance to get on stage if you haven’t proven yourself, and you can’t prove yourself unless you get on stage. It’s so sad. Columbus is much better for drag than Cleveland is, there’s always something or someone doing a show here, and Axis is very good for that, they’re very good for making shows, making sure that all the queens that work for them can make money and say sustained in this screwed up time. And Cleveland needs that too, Cleveland has a bevy of great entertainers.


    LEILA: Are you still in touch with the Cleveland community from back then?


    ANDRIA: Oh yeah. Those are friends I made in my twenties and they’re still my friends now. And I keep in contact with everybody. Like Kari Nickels—she was always the mainstay in the community, and always made money, and people always flocked to her shows. And when Bounce closed for good and they didn’t need her anymore, it’s so sad, so sad. If I had the money, I would throw it into that place in a minute.


      Adaptations + Challenges of COVID-19

    ANDRIA: With Corona around, I hate to say this, but I think that the time of going to sit down and see shows and enjoy shows like we used to, has come and gone. This is the harbinger of what it’s become, and how we’re going to have to change our whole way of entertainment, and how we’re going to convey drag and everything else. I mean down here in Columbus, you have to wear a full-face shield, there’s no hand-to-hand tipping, people can’t get out of their seats, it’s very sterile and very—ugh. It’s like doing drag in a study hall room. It’s very, very weird. And it’s taken the intimacy out of it. I’ve always liked that—being on stage and being able to look into somebody’s face, and get close and personal with them, and talk to them. You know, the one-on-one. And you can’t have that now… This is new territory for everybody. And they’ve never, not in my lifetime, have they ever enforced quarantine, has anyone been this vigilant about an airborne pathogen.

    With all drag, drag changes. There’s an ebb and flow, different styles of drag change. I have my own Facebook Live show, me and Maria, and that’s how I’ve been fulfilling my drag outlet and making money doing what I like to do—which is being a talker. I get on there and talk like an idiot and say things that I shouldn’t, but nevertheless people enjoy it, so I do it anyway. Girls are doing numbers on Facebook and on Instagram, doing that in their living room, and people tip on Cash App and Venmo. I don’t think that’s going to go away anytime soon. If anything, I think it’s even going to get more involved with that. So that people don’t have to leave the comfort of their home to watch a drag show. [Note: Andria's Cash App handle is $Andria1000]


    LEILA: Just seeing these things take virtual form that we never imagined would have to be virtual.


    ANDRIA: No! Nobody. And I’m telling you, especially being an MC, when I have to sit in a room with nobody other than my roommate, and I’m talking, I can’t tell whether what I’m saying lands or whether it falls flat, because there’s no participation from the audience, there’s no energy from anybody else in the room, because I’m the only person there. So I don’t know if it’s funny, I don’t know if it’s bad, I don’t know if it’s offensive, I don’t know anything. I just have to talk and hope that it’s funny and hope that people are enjoying it and hope that people are laughing. And I can’t tell whether people are enjoying it until it’s over with, so I can check the comments. So it’s weird, it’s really weird.


      Intimacy and Community

    ANDRIA: You know, the thing about Bounce… the whole time I was there, there was never a lull in the crowd, in the people that came. I will tell you this; you did see a color switch in the clientele at the five- or six-year mark. It got a lot darker, which I enjoy—I’m Black myself, so enjoyed that. It got a lot darker, and then it leveled out. Bounce integrated a lot of the Black and white community together. Something that most cities in Ohio are still lacking. It was plagued by some of the violent side of Cleveland, you know, the gay community in Cleveland tends to be a little bit more aggressive than most, which I enjoy, again, because I’m a hot ass mess [laughs].


    LEILA: No shame!


    ANDRIA: Girl, I could tell you stories from my youth that would make your skin crawl. But it was refreshing, because like I said, it was a safe place for everyone to go to and enjoy their craft. There was a big boom in female impersonation when Bounce first opened. There were a lot of girls that were more comfortable, just like, ‘oh there’s a place for me to go, Andria will always make sure that I’ll get on and do some kind of show.’ And I had a huge pool of entertainment to choose from when working those shows. There was a never a loss for girls, never. Which was good for me, but it was good for them too! It gave them a chance to prove their chops while they were on stage. And it was a good time, it was a really good time. And it brought together the community, I will say that. Bounce had a really good sense of community, and it hired almost exclusively from the community, so everyone that worked there was gay. They were good to work for because it’s gay-owned and operated, and they like to take care of their employees. I met some great people the whole time I was working there. I lost a few friends along the way, but that tends to happen with our lifestyle. You lived hard, you die fast. And that was another thing. There’s two types of people in the gay community—there are those who are responsible for the party, and those who enjoy the party. And those who are responsible for the party, party hard. Because without us, nobody else can enjoy their time. I’m an entertainer, people were coming to the bar to see me entertain. So we had a good time and did a lot of good time drugs, and did a lot of good time cocktails, not that anybody needs them, but that didn’t stop me. It was just par for the course then.


      Image courtesy of Denny Petkovsek

    When Bounce was open, it made you feel good about who you are, made you feel good about what was going on. Because there was always something to do, you always met your friends there, you could be friends with an eclectic group of people. Especially for me, I could go there and say hi to 50,000 people and never even get close to the middle of the bar. It takes you 45 minutes to walk through the place, and you were supposed to be there to work 20 minutes ago. And it’s like oh my god it’s too crowded, everyone was talking to me. It really made you feel like you were a star. And everybody felt that way when they walked in there. It was a big version of Cheers. It was wonderful, it was an absolutely wonderful place, and it did a lot for the community. It brought straight, gay, lesbian—which is another thing—a lot of lesbians have their own subculture, gay men have their subculture, Black gay men, Black gay women. Bounce brought everyone together, it didn’t matter, did not matter. It was such a good place for that.


    LEILA: That’s so special. I feel like that’s so rare now. I think my generation is more isolated as a whole, so hearing about the joy and sense of community that space brought folks just reaffirms to me how important and necessary it was.


    ANDRIA: And I attribute the loss of it to the internet. The internet and apps on your phone. Let’s be honest, people go out for Dick Drugs Dancing and Drinks—that’s what they go out for—the four D’s. They can do drugs at home, they can find dick on their phone, they can dance on their phone, and they can do other things on their phone. They have no reason to go out anymore! Bars don’t have that mass appeal like they did.


    LEILA: Oh my god you’re right, with dating apps now too. That makes me hope that after this pandemic runs its course, people would be so bored from being alone and on their phones that we’d be ready to have another revival of that.


    ANDRIA: I’m hoping that if they get a grip on this pandemic, that people will be so happy to get up out of their houses that they will flood the bars. That would be nice.
